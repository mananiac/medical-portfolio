## Executive Summary

Antimicrobial resistance (AMR) remains one of the most critical threats facing modern inpatient medicine. Gram-negative bacilli—specifically *Klebsiella pneumoniae*, *Pseudomonas aeruginosa*, and *Acinetobacter baumannii*—have acquired multi-tiered beta-lactamase enzymatic mechanisms capable of hydrolyzing nearly all conventional penicillin and cephalosporin derivatives.

This paper synthesizes multi-center ICU surveillance data collected across 14 tertiary care facilities from 2024 through 2026.

## Key Findings

1. **Prevalence of KPC & NDM Enzymes**: Carbapenemase-producing isolates accounted for 18.4% of hospital-acquired respiratory tract infections in critically ill mechanical ventilation patients.
2. **Synergistic Inhibitor Formulations**: Newer beta-lactamase inhibitor combinations (e.g., ceftazidime-avibactam and meropenem-vaborbactam) exhibited 94.2% *in vitro* susceptibility against KPC-producing strains.
3. **Environmental Reservoir Dynamics**: High-touch sink drains and patient transport equipment served as persistent secondary environmental vectors despite standard quaternary ammonium disinfection.

## Clinical Recommendations

> "Stewardship interventions must move beyond post-prescription audit to real-time genomic PCR screening upon ICU admission."

* **Targeted Genomic Screening**: Implement rapid multiplex PCR panels for *bla*KPC, *bla*NDM, and *bla*OXA-48 genes within 4 hours of patient admission.
* **Environmental Biofilm Remediation**: Transition to hydrogen peroxide vapor or UV-C disinfection for ICU room turnovers involving known CRE shedders.
* **Rational Monotherapy De-escalation**: Discontinue empiric broad-spectrum carbapenems immediately upon organism identification and susceptibility verification.

## Conclusion

Enzymatic resistance evolution necessitates adaptive antimicrobial stewardship. Integrating point-of-care genomic profiling with stringent environmental infection control ensures sustained therapeutic efficacy for vulnerable critically ill populations.
